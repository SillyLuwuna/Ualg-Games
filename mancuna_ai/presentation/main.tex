\section{Problem Analysis}

\begin{frame}
    \begin{beamercolorbox}[sep=8pt,center,rounded=true,shadow=true]{title}
		\usebeamerfont{title}\Huge\textbf{Problem Analysis}
    \end{beamercolorbox}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem Analysis}
	\begin{center}
		\begin{table}
			\begin{center}
				\begin{tabular}{| c | c |}
					2 & 2\\
					\hline
					\hline
					2 & 2\\
				\end{tabular}
				\caption{Starting Mancuna board}
			\end{center}
		\end{table}
	\end{center}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Board is 2x2.
		\item Pieces move counter-clockwise.
		\item There is a total of 8 pieces.
		\item Tie repetition length of 3.
		\item Maximum of two possible moves each turn.
		\item If there are no legal moves, the opponent wins by default.
		\item The score is based on how many pieces are in each player's board.
	\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem Analysis}
	When looking at the search space abstractly we can infer certain information.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item There are about $\sim 150$ possible board combinations.
		\item Considering who makes the next move, there are about $\sim 300$ possible board combinations
	\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem Analysis}
	Looking at the search space during all possible match combinations,
	some more interesting conclusions can be drawn.

	\bigskip

	During a match there are only \textbf{113 possible states}, taking into account who makes the next move.

	\bigskip

	With a branching factor of 2 at most, the search space \textbf{seems extremely small}.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem Analysis}
	In reality such is not the case.

	\bigskip

	The tie repetition rule makes it so that each branch in the search space is different: \textbf{caching is not possible}.

	\bigskip

	Because caching is not possible, and given that there are tie loops of over 30 plays long,
	the search space quickly becomes over $2^{30}$ which \textbf{exceeds a billion different branches}.
\end{frame}



\section{Best Option AI}

\begin{frame}
    \begin{beamercolorbox}[sep=8pt,center,rounded=true,shadow=true]{title}
		\usebeamerfont{title}\Huge\textbf{Best Option AI}
    \end{beamercolorbox}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Option AI}
	The idea is to implement a minmax-like algorithm while disregarding the score and
	looking only at \textbf{the worst-case outcome} for a play.

	\bigskip

	When looking at the search space in this manner, \textbf{caching becomes possible again}.

	\bigskip

	This means that the minmax must \textbf{at most calculate 113 states}, at which point it
	already knows the entire search space.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Option AI}
	Upon having the worst-case outcome for every single possible board, the AI
	executes the following heuristic.

	\bigskip

	\begin{itemize}
		\item If there is only one legal move, it will play it blindly.
		\item If one of the moves has the worst-case outcome of a win, that move will be played.
		\item If no winning moves exist, the first move with the worst-case outcome as a tie will be played.
		\item If there are no winning or tying moves, the first move is played.
	\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Option AI}
	\begin{center}
		\huge \textbf{Results}
	\end{center}

	\bigskip

	On both sides of the board this AI ties with the expert AI, achieving a score of 5-3 regardless of
	going first or second.

	\bigskip

	On the first move for both sides of the board, this AI has at least one move which has a worst-case
	outcome of a tie.

	\bigskip

	This means that this is a \textbf{complete game} where only ties
	are possible when both players play optimally.
\end{frame}

\section{Best Score AI}

\begin{frame}
    \begin{beamercolorbox}[sep=8pt,center,rounded=true,shadow=true]{title}
		\usebeamerfont{title}\Huge\textbf{Best Score AI}
    \end{beamercolorbox}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	As explained eariler, we cannot do a blind search across the entire search space.

	\bigskip

	However, we can \textbf{exploit the Best Option AI} to search only through a very small
	portion of the entire search space.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	\textbf{Whenever the Best Option AI is in a situation where both options lead to a tie,
	we can execute minmax through the search space considering only the tying moves.}

	\bigskip

	With this, we get a \textbf{worst-case tying score} for each move.

	\bigskip

	Upon having this score, this AI behaves exactly like the Best Option AI, however,
	when both options are ties, it will choose the one that is guaranteed a higher score.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	\begin{center}
		\huge \textbf{Results}
	\end{center}

	\bigskip

	When playing \textbf{first}, it ties \textbf{4-4} with the expert AI.

	When playing \textbf{second}, it ties \textbf{5-3} with the expert AI.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	When playing optimally, the \textbf{first} player can only tie with a score of \textbf{at most 3}.
	While the \textbf{second} player can only tie with a score of \textbf{at most 5}.

	\bigskip

	With this, we can conclude that \textbf{the game is solved} and the \textbf{Best Score AI plays optimally}.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	\begin{center}
		\huge \textbf{If it plays optimally, why does it get a lower score against the expert AI than the "dumber" Best Option AI?}
	\end{center}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Best Score AI}
	The reason for the "smarter" AI to perform worst is that it \textbf{assumes both players
	play optimally}.

	\bigskip

	The expert AI does not play optimally. And as such it has patterns that can be exploited.

	\bigskip

	\textbf{By cheer luck}, the Best Option AI, as it plays double tying moves blindly, plays a pattern
	in which the expert AI \textbf{makes mistakes} and ends up tying with a higher score.

	\bigskip

	As the Best Score AI only plays optimally, and by \textbf{cheer luck} in the optimal play the expert
	AI \textbf{does not make the same mistakes}, it ends up with a lower score. Even then the expert AI makes one mistake,
	which leads the Best Score AI to be able to achieve a score of 4-4 instead of the expected 3-5.
\end{frame}

\section{Final AI and remarks}

\begin{frame}
    \begin{beamercolorbox}[sep=8pt,center,rounded=true,shadow=true]{title}
		\usebeamerfont{title}\Huge\textbf{Final AI and remarks}
    \end{beamercolorbox}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Final AI and remarks}
	The final AI used was the \textbf{Best Option AI}. Although worse, it performs better for the competition.

	\bigskip

	\textbf{For playing optimally} the Best Score AI would be used, as it plays optimally.

	\bigskip

	\textbf{For the competition}, an AI could be developed that specifically exploits the expert AI.
	With that strategy it could be \textbf{theoretically possible to achieve higher scores or even win against
	the expert AI}, depending on if it has any path of play in which it makes worse blunders.
\end{frame}
